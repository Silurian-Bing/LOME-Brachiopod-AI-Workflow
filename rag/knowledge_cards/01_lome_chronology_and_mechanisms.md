# Knowledge Card 01: LOME Chronology and Mechanisms

## Scope

This card summarizes the chronology and mechanism literature that should anchor RAG answers about the Late Ordovician mass extinction.

## Core Claims

High-precision dating now supports a short and rapidly paced extinction interval. The Science Advances chronology constrains the onset of LOME near 442.76 Ma and frames the two main phases as events with sharply different durations and rates of climate change. This is important for the workflow because stage-level paleobiogeographic bins must be treated as coarse summaries over a much shorter crisis interval, rather than as exact ecological time slices (`zhang_etal_2025_lome_tempo`).

The classic two-phase framework remains essential. The first pulse is commonly linked to cooling, glaciation, sea-level fall, and habitat loss on epicontinental shelves; the second pulse is commonly linked to deglaciation, sea-level rise, warming, and expanded anoxia/euxinia (`sheehan_2001_lome`; `finnegan_etal_2011_glaciation`; `zou_etal_2018_double_whammy`).

Redox mechanisms should be represented as spatially and temporally heterogeneous. Local euxinia, ferruginous anoxia, global proxy behavior, and rapid oxygen variability do not always imply a single globally uniform ocean state. RAG answers should therefore avoid phrases such as "global anoxia caused everything" unless the cited source and spatial scale are specified (`hammarlund_etal_2012_sulfidic`; `kozik_etal_2022_redox`; `dahl_etal_2021_mo_u`).

Climate modeling shows that ice-sheet growth and temperature change need not be synchronous. This supports a workflow design in which environmental context, age models, and fossil occurrence patterns are treated as separate evidence layers rather than merged into one causal narrative (`pohl_etal_2016_glacial_onset`).

## Review Gate

Any statement claiming a single dominant kill mechanism must be checked by the review agent. Mechanism claims are disputed and proxy-dependent.
